PwC / NCG Client · Executive Dashboards · Decision Design · 04/05

PWC DASHBOARD Portfolio Intelligence for Executive Decision-Making.

Role

Lead UX Designer — end-to-end

Context

Internal dashboard · NCG client · Power BI

Users

CEO Office · C-suite · Senior leadership

Scale

96 projects · ₹99.57M portfolio

01Context

DATA WITHOUT HIERARCHY IS NOISE.

PwC was managing a portfolio of 96 active projects across Banking, Insurance, Retail, Hospitality, and Public Services. The organisation had data. What it didn't have was a coherent way for leadership to read that portfolio as a whole — to understand health, flag risk, track accountability, and make decisions before problems compounded.

I led UX for a productivity dashboard that replaced fragmented reporting with a unified decision surface for PwC's CEO Office.

The Brief

"Design a dashboard that gives leadership visibility into project progress, budget performance, and team productivity."

Standard brief. The design problem underneath it was not standard.

02What Was Actually Wrong

TWO DIFFERENT COGNITIVE TASKS.

The failure mode of most reporting dashboards is not that they show the wrong data. It's that they show data without hierarchy — everything visible, nothing prioritised, the user left to form their own interpretation from scratch every time.

At PwC, when a budget overrun emerged, it was discovered in a meeting — not in a monitoring system. The problem wasn't the absence of data. It was the absence of a decision layer.

A DASHBOARD DESIGNED FOR AN ANALYST AND ONE DESIGNED FOR A C-SUITE EXECUTIVE ARE DIFFERENT PRODUCTS. THE ANALYST INVESTIGATES. THE EXECUTIVE DECIDES.

03Key Design Decisions

FIVE DECISIONS THAT MADE IT A DECISION SURFACE.

Four questions every executive asks — every panel maps to one

Is the portfolio healthy?

Projects Health · KPI strip

Are we on track financially?

Estimated vs Actual · Budget vs Expenses

Where is capacity deployed?

Strategic Alignment · Effort chart

Who is driving results?

Leaderboard · Project by PMs

Decision 01

The KPI Strip as a Decision Trigger Layer

Five metrics at the top — not summary statistics. A trigger layer.

The directional signal attached to each metric — trend arrow + percentage delta — transforms KPIs from numbers into questions: is this moving in the right direction? The relationship between Total Budget (₹99.57M) and Total Expenses (₹109.66M) is legible at a glance, without requiring the user to compare two separate panels.

Design target: a budget overrun visible in 3 seconds rather than discovered in a meeting. Not accuracy. Immediacy.

Decision 02

Risk Classification Over Status Tracking

Status models undersurface problems. Project teams self-report status, and self-reported status is almost always optimistic until a problem is unavoidable.

Projects rated Low Risk (40%), Medium Risk (32%), or High Risk (28%) — by a risk profile including budget variance, timeline trajectory, and dependency exposure. A project on track today but showing budget variance and blocked dependencies is Low Risk on a status model and Medium Risk on a risk model. The risk model catches it earlier.

Decision 03

Estimated vs Actual as a Time-Series, Not a Snapshot

A single-period comparison can always be explained away. A 12-month trend line makes the pattern visible — whether actuals have been consistently above estimates, whether the gap is widening, whether a spike is anomalous or recurring.

For an organisation of PwC's scale, showing the trend rather than the moment puts the quality of forecasting itself under review, not just the current variance.

Decision 04

The Leaderboard as Accountability — Not Pressure

Top 5 PMs ranked by revenue, quarterly (not running total). Quarterly framing smooths short-term variance. Leadership already knows who the top performers are — the dashboard makes that shared knowledge legible and consistent rather than based on whoever was visible in the last meeting.

Decision 05

Strategic Alignment — Where Is the Organisation Investing?

Projects represented by business domain — Banking, Insurance, Retail, Hospitality, Public Services — surfacing a view the operational panels don't provide. This panel only exists because early conversations surfaced that "where are we investing?" was a question leadership couldn't answer from existing reports without requesting a manual analysis. Putting it on the primary view changed when the question got asked.

The Full Dashboard — All Five Decisions in One View

KPI Trigger Layer

Total Budget ₹99.57M vs Total Expenses ₹109.66M — the overrun is legible in the same scan as the count. Directional arrows show trend direction without requiring the user to compare separate panels.

Risk vs Status

Projects Health pie shows 40% Low / 32% Medium / 28% High — risk-classified by budget variance and trajectory, not self-reported delivery status. 60% of the portfolio at some risk level is a portfolio-level signal.

Every Panel Earns Its Place

Estimated vs Actual Cost (12-month trend), Strategic Alignment by domain (Banking 17, Insurance 12…), Leaderboard quarterly, Project by PMs — every panel maps to one of the four executive questions. Nothing on-screen because data was available.

05Learnings

REPORTS GIVE INFORMATION. DASHBOARDS SUPPORT DECISIONS.

The discipline in this project was reduction. Every panel, every metric, every chart had to answer: what decision does this support? Not "is this data available?" Not "would an analyst find this useful?"

The Budget vs Expenses relationship took longer to get right than any individual chart — because the challenge wasn't showing both numbers, it was making the relationship between them legible at a glance, with trend direction visible immediately, without requiring the user to do arithmetic.

THAT LEVEL OF PRECISION IN INFORMATION HIERARCHY IS WHAT SEPARATES A DASHBOARD FROM A REPORT.
